# Concerning Nibbāna (Fourth Discourse)
## Catutthanibbānapaṭisaṁyuttasutta (Ud 8.4)

> Evaṁ me sutaṁ—
> ekaṁ samayaṁ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṁ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme.
> Tena kho pana samayena bhagavā bhikkhū nibbānapaṭisaṁyuttāya dhammiyā kathāya sandasseti samādapeti samuttejeti sampahaṁseti.
> Tedha bhikkhū aṭṭhiṁ katvā manasi katvā sabbaṁ cetaso samannāharitvā ohitasotā dhammaṁ suṇanti.

Thus have I heard—
At one time the Blessed One was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's monastery.
At that time, the Blessed One instructed, encouraged, inspired, and gladdened the monks with a talk on the Dhamma (*dhamma*) concerning final liberation (*nibbāna*).
And those monks, paying thorough attention, taking it to heart, and concentrating with their whole mind, listened to the Dhamma with rapt attention.

> Atha kho bhagavā etamatthaṁ viditvā tāyaṁ velāyaṁ imaṁ udānaṁ udānesi:
> 
> “Nissitassa calitaṁ, anissitassa calitaṁ natthi.
> Calite asati passaddhi,
> passaddhiyā sati nati na hoti.
> Natiyā asati āgatigati na hoti.
> Āgatigatiyā asati cutūpapāto na hoti.
> Cutūpapāte asati nevidha na huraṁ na ubhayamantarena.
> Esevanto dukkhassā”ti.

Then the Blessed One, understanding the significance of this, uttered this inspired utterance at that time:

"For one who clings, there is wavering; for one who does not cling, there is no wavering.
When there is no wavering, there is stillness;
when there is stillness, there is no inclination.
When there is no inclination, there is no coming and going.
When there is no coming and going, there is no passing away and rebirth.
When there is no passing away and rebirth, there is neither a 'here' nor a 'there' nor anything in between.
This, indeed, is the end of suffering (*dukkha*)."

## Glossary

| Pāli (IAST) | Target transliteration | Target gloss | First-chapter occurrence | Goenka-tradition usage |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **nibbāna** | nibbāna | final liberation | Nibbāna Sutta | The ultimate state of peace and liberation from all suffering, attained by extinguishing the fires of craving, aversion, and ignorance. |
| **dhamma** | Dhamma | the teaching; the universal law | Nibbāna Sutta | Capitalized to denote the universal law of nature and the path taught by the Buddha. |
| **calita** | calita | wavering | Nibbāna Sutta | The instability or movement of the mind when it reacts to sensory objects with craving or aversion. |
| **passaddhi** | passaddhi | stillness; tranquility | Nibbāna Sutta | The deep tranquility and calmness of the mind when it is free from wavering and reactions. |
| **nati** | nati | inclination; bending | Nibbāna Sutta | The bending or leaning of the mind towards objects of attachment, generating subsequent reactions. |
| **dukkha** | dukkha | suffering | Nibbāna Sutta | The inherent unsatisfactoriness of conditioned existence. |

## Translator Notes

1. **Intimate Teacher-to-Student Voice**: The English translation maintains a direct and experiential tone, translating the Buddha's concise statement with simple and powerful language that reflects the meditative process of self-observation.
2. **Wavering (*Calita*) and Stillness (*Passaddhi*)**: Translated *calita* as "wavering" to represent the unstable, reactive nature of the mind when it clings (*nissita*). The opposite, *passaddhi*, is translated as "stillness" to denote the deep, non-reactive peace of a mind established in equanimity.
3. **Inclination (*Nati*)**: Rendered as "inclination" to capture the subtle mental bending or leaning of the mind towards or away from sensations before full-blown craving and aversion manifest.
4. **Coming and Going (*Āgatigati*) and Passing Away and Rebirth (*Cutūpapāto*)**: Metaphorically, these represent the macro-cycle of rebirth in samsara, and phenomenologically, they describe the micro-cycle of the arising and passing away of conditioned mental states.
