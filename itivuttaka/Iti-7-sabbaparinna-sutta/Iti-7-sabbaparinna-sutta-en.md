# Full Understanding of the All
## Sabbapariññā Sutta (Iti 7)

> Vuttañhetaṁ bhagavatā vuttamarahatāti me sutaṁ:
> 
> “Sabbaṁ, bhikkhave, anabhijānaṁ aparijānaṁ tattha cittaṁ avirājayaṁ appajahaṁ abhabbo dukkhakkhayāya.
> Sabbañca kho, bhikkhave, abhijānaṁ parijānaṁ tattha cittaṁ virājayaṁ pajahaṁ bhabbo dukkhakkhayāyā”ti.

This was said by the Blessed One, said by the Arahant, as I have heard:

"Monks, without directly knowing and fully understanding everything, without detaching the mind from it and abandoning it, one is incapable of ending suffering.
But, monks, by directly knowing and fully understanding everything, detaching the mind from it and abandoning it, one is capable of ending suffering."

> Etamatthaṁ bhagavā avoca.
> Tatthetaṁ iti vuccati:

The Blessed One said this.
In this connection, this was said:

> “Yo sabbaṁ sabbato ñatvā,
> sabbathesu na rajjati;
> Sa ve sabbapariññā so,
> sabbadukkhamupaccagā”ti.

"One who has known the All from all around,
and is not stirred by passion for anything at all:
having fully understood the All,
he has gone beyond all suffering."

> Ayampi attho vutto bhagavatā, iti me sutanti.
> 
> Sattamaṁ.

This matter too was spoken by the Blessed One, as I have heard.

(Seventh)

| **sabba** | sabba | the All; everything | Full Understanding of the All | Expounded by the Buddha as the six internal sense bases (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind) and their corresponding six external objects. |

## Translator Notes

1. **The All (*Sabba*)**: In the *Sabba Sutta* (SN 35.23), the Buddha defines "the All" as the six senses and their objects. Nothing exists outside this experiential framework, and this is where Vipassana practice is applied.
2. **Direct and Full Knowing (*Abhijāna* / *Parijāna*)**: Represents two levels of insight: *abhijā* is the initial direct observation of sensations, and *parijā* is the mature, comprehensive understanding of their impermanent nature (*anicca*).
3. **Fading of Passion (*Virāga*)**: Rather than a forced suppression, *virāga* is the natural consequence of observing sensations with equanimity, which automatically cools the fire of reaction.
