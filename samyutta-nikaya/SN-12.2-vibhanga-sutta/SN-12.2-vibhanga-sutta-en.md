# The Discourse on Analysis
## Vibhaṅgasutta (SN 12.2)

> Sāvatthiyaṁ viharati.
> 
> “Paṭiccasamuppādaṁ vo, bhikkhave, desessāmi vibhajissāmi.
> Taṁ suṇātha, sādhukaṁ manasi karotha, bhāsissāmī”ti.

Staying near Sāvatthī.

“Monks, I will teach you dependent origination (*paṭiccasamuppāda*) and analyze it for you. Listen, pay close attention, and I will speak.”

> “Evaṁ, bhante”ti kho te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṁ.
> Bhagavā etadavoca:

“Yes, Bhante,” those monks replied to the Blessed One.
The Blessed One said this:

> “Katamo ca, bhikkhave, paṭiccasamuppādo?
> Avijjāpaccayā, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā;
> saṅkhārapaccayā viññāṇaṁ;
> viññāṇapaccayā nāmarūpaṁ;
> nāmarūpapaccayā saḷāyatanaṁ;
> saḷāyatanapaccayā phasso;
> phassapaccayā vedanā;
> vedanāpaccayā taṇhā;
> taṇhāpaccayā upādānaṁ;
> upādānapaccayā bhavo;
> bhavapaccayā jāti;
> jātipaccayā jarāmaraṇaṁ sokaparidevadukkhadomanassupāyāsā sambhavanti.
> Evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa samudayo hoti.

“And what, monks, is dependent origination?
With ignorance (*avijjā*) as condition, mental conditioning (*saṅkhāra*s) arise;
with mental conditioning as condition, consciousness (*viññāṇa*) arises;
with consciousness as condition, mind-and-matter (*nāmarūpa*) arises;
with mind-and-matter as condition, the six sense doors (*saḷāyatana*) arise;
with the six sense doors as condition, contact (*phassa*) arises;
with contact as condition, sensations (*vedanā*s) arise;
with sensations as condition, craving (*taṇhā*) arises;
with craving as condition, clinging (*upādāna*) arises;
with clinging as condition, becoming (*bhava*) arises;
with becoming as condition, birth (*jāti*) arises;
with birth as condition, decay and death (*jarāmaraṇa*), sorrow, lamentation, physical pain, mental grief, and despair arise.
Thus is the origin of this entire mass of suffering.

> Katamañca, bhikkhave, jarāmaraṇaṁ?
> Yā tesaṁ tesaṁ sattānaṁ tamhi tamhi sattanikāye jarā jīraṇatā khaṇḍiccaṁ pāliccaṁ valittacatā āyuno saṁhāni indriyānaṁ paripāko;
> ayaṁ vuccati jarā.
> Yā tesaṁ tesaṁ sattānaṁ tamhā tamhā sattanikāyā cuti cavanatā bhedo antaradhānaṁ maccu maraṇaṁ kālakiriyā khandhānaṁ bhedo kaḷevarassa nikkhepo (…),
> idaṁ vuccati maraṇaṁ.
> Iti ayañca jarā, idañca maraṇaṁ.
> Idaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, jarāmaraṇaṁ.

And what, monks, is decay and death?
The decay of various beings in their respective categories of beings—their growing old, the breaking of their teeth, the graying of their hair, the wrinkling of their skin, the decline of their life span, and the wearing out of their senses; this is called decay.
The passing away of various beings from their respective categories of beings—their falling away, breaking up, disappearance, dying, death, completion of time, the breaking up of the aggregates, and the laying down of the body; this is called death.
So this decay and this death are what is called decay and death.

> Katamā ca, bhikkhave, jāti?
> Yā tesaṁ tesaṁ sattānaṁ tamhi tamhi sattanikāye jāti sañjāti okkanti abhinibbatti khandhānaṁ pātubhāvo āyatanānaṁ paṭilābho.
> Ayaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, jāti.

And what, monks, is birth?
The birth of various beings in their respective categories of beings—their production, descent, arising, the appearance of the aggregates, and the acquisition of the sense doors. This is called birth.

> Katamo ca, bhikkhave, bhavo?
> Tayome, bhikkhave, bhavā—
> kāmabhavo, rūpabhavo, arūpabhavo.
> Ayaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, bhavo.

And what, monks, is becoming?
There are these three kinds of becoming:
sensual becoming (*kāmabhava*), fine-material becoming (*rūpabhava*), and immaterial becoming (*arūpabhava*).
This is called becoming.

> Katamañca, bhikkhave, upādānaṁ?
> Cattārimāni, bhikkhave, upādānāni—
> kāmupādānaṁ, diṭṭhupādānaṁ, sīlabbatupādānaṁ, attavādupādānaṁ.
> Idaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, upādānaṁ.

And what, monks, is clinging?
There are these four kinds of clinging:
clinging to sensual pleasures (*kāmupādāna*), clinging to views (*diṭṭhupādāna*), clinging to rules and rituals (*sīlabbatupādāna*), and clinging to a belief in self (*attavādupādāna*).
This is called clinging.

> Katamā ca, bhikkhave, taṇhā?
> Chayime, bhikkhave, taṇhākāyā—
> rūpataṇhā, saddataṇhā, gandhataṇhā, rasatahā, phoṭṭhabbataṇhā, dhammataṇhā.
> Ayaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, taṇhā.

And what, monks, is craving?
There are these six groups of craving:
craving for sights (*rūpataṇhā*), craving for sounds (*saddataṇhā*), craving for smells (*gandhataṇhā*), craving for tastes (*rasataṇhā*), craving for touches (*phoṭṭhabbataṇhā*), and craving for thoughts (*dhammataṇhā*).
This is called craving.

> Katamā ca, bhikkhave, vedanā?
> Chayime, bhikkhave, vedanākāyā—
> cakkhusamphassajā vedanā, sotasamphassajā vedanā, ghānasamphassajā vedanā, jivhāsamphassajā vedanā, kāyasamphassajā vedanā, manosamphassajā vedanā.
> Ayaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, vedanā.

And what, monks, is sensation?
There are these six groups of sensations:
sensations born of eye-contact (*cakkhusamphassajā vedanā*), sensations born of ear-contact (*sotasamphassajā vedanā*), sensations born of nose-contact (*ghānasamphassajā vedanā*), sensations born of tongue-contact (*jivhāsamphassajā vedanā*), sensations born of body-contact (*kāyasamphassajā vedanā*), and sensations born of mind-contact (*manosamphassajā vedanā*).
This is called sensation.

> Katamo ca, bhikkhave, phasso?
> Chayime, bhikkhave, phassakāyā—
> cakkhusamphasso, sotasamphasso, ghānasamphasso, jivhāsamphasso, kāyasamphasso, manosamphasso.
> Ayaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, phasso.

And what, monks, is contact?
There are these six groups of contact:
eye-contact (*cakkhusamphasso*), ear-contact (*sotasamphasso*), nose-contact (*ghānasamphasso*), tongue-contact (*jivhāsamphasso*), body-contact (*kāyasamphasso*), and mind-contact (*manosamphasso*).
This is called contact.

> Katamañca, bhikkhave, saḷāyatanaṁ?
> Cakkhāyatanaṁ, sotāyatanaṁ, ghānāyatanaṁ, jivhāyatanaṁ, kāyāyatanaṁ, manāyatanaṁ—
> idaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, saḷāyatanaṁ.

And what, monks, are the six sense doors?
The eye door (*cakkhāyatana*), ear door (*sotāyatana*), nose door (*ghānāyatana*), tongue door (*jivhāyatana*), body door (*kāyāyatana*), and mind door (*manāyatana*)—these are called the six sense doors.

> Katamañca, bhikkhave, nāmarūpaṁ?
> Vedanā, saññā, cetanā, phasso, manasikāro—
> idaṁ vuccati nāmaṁ.
> Cattāro ca mahābhūtā, catunnañca mahābhūtānaṁ upādāyarūpaṁ.
> Idaṁ vuccati rūpaṁ.
> Iti idañca nāmaṁ, idañca rūpaṁ.
> Idaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, nāmarūpaṁ.

And what, monks, is mind-and-matter?
Sensations (*vedanā*), perception (*saññā*), volition (*cetanā*), contact (*phassa*), and attention (*manasikāra*)—this is called mind (*nāma*).
The four primary elements (*mahābhūtā*) and the matter derived from them (*upādāyarūpa*)—this is called matter (*rūpa*).
So this is mind and this is matter; this is what is called mind-and-matter.

> Katamañca, bhikkhave, viññāṇaṁ?
> Chayime, bhikkhave, viññāṇakāyā—
> cakkhuviññāṇaṁ, sotaviññāṇaṁ, ghānaviññāṇaṁ, jivhāviññāṇaṁ, kāyaviññāṇaṁ, manoviññāṇaṁ.
> Idaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, viññāṇaṁ.

And what, monks, is consciousness?
There are these six groups of consciousness:
eye-consciousness (*cakkhuviññāṇa*), ear-consciousness (*sotaviññāṇa*), nose-consciousness (*ghānaviññāṇa*), tongue-consciousness (*jivhāviññāṇa*), body-consciousness (*kāyaviññāṇa*), and mind-consciousness (*manoviññāṇa*).
This is called consciousness.

> Kateme ca, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā?
> Tayome, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā—
> kāyasaṅkhāro, vacīsaṅkhāro, cittasaṅkhāro.
> Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā.

And what, monks, is mental conditioning?
There are these three kinds of mental conditioning:
bodily conditioning (*kāyasaṅkhāra*), verbal conditioning (*vacīsaṅkhāra*), and mental conditioning (*cittasaṅkhāra*).
These are called mental conditioning.

> Katamā ca, bhikkhave, avijjā?
> Yaṁ kho, bhikkhave, dukkhe aññāṇaṁ, dukkhasamudaye aññāṇaṁ, dukkhanirodhe aññāṇaṁ, dukkhanirodhagāminiyā paṭipadāya aññāṇaṁ.
> Ayaṁ vuccati, bhikkhave, avijjā.

And what, monks, is ignorance?
Not knowing suffering (*dukkha*), not knowing the origin of suffering, not knowing the cessation of suffering, and not knowing the path leading to the cessation of suffering. This is called ignorance.

> Iti kho, bhikkhave, avijjāpaccayā saṅkhārā;
> 
> saṅkhārapaccayā viññāṇaṁ …pe…
> evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa samudayo hoti.
> Avijjāya tveva asesavirāganirodhā saṅkhāranirodho;
> saṅkhāranirodhā viññāṇanirodho …pe…
> evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa nirodho hotī”ti.

“Thus, monks, with ignorance as condition, mental conditioning arises;
with mental conditioning as condition, consciousness... and so on...
Thus is the origin of this entire mass of suffering.
But with the complete fading away and cessation of ignorance, mental conditioning ceases;
with the cessation of mental conditioning, consciousness ceases... and so on...
Thus is the cessation of this entire mass of suffering.”

> Dutiyaṁ.

(Second)

## Translator Notes

1. **Intimate Teacher-to-Student Voice**: The English translation maintains a direct and experiential tone, matching Goenkaji's spoken style.
2. **Mind-and-Matter (`nāmarūpa`)**: Translated as "mind-and-matter" in accordance with the target language profile's guidelines. Inside `nāma`, we specify the mental aggregates (*vedanā*, *saññā*, *cetanā*, *phassa*, *manasikāra*) and distinguish them from the physical aggregate (*rūpa*).
3. **Six Sense Doors (`saḷāyatana`)**: Rendered as "six sense doors" to highlight their function in meditation as the contact points where sensations (*vedanā*) arise.
4. **Sense-Contact Sensations**: Maintained strict grammatical parallelism in translating the six categories of sensations (*cakkhusamphassajā vedanā*), craving, and contact, illustrating the chain of dependent origination.
