# A Fork in the Road
## Dvidhāpathasutta (Ud 8.7)

> Evaṁ me sutaṁ—
> ekaṁ samayaṁ bhagavā kosalesu addhānamaggappaṭipanno hoti āyasmatā nāgasamālena pacchāsamaṇena.
> Addasā kho āyasmā nāgasamālo antarāmagge dvidhāpathaṁ.
> Disvāna bhagavantaṁ etadavoca:
> “ayaṁ, bhante, bhagavā pantho;
> iminā gacchāmā”ti.
> Evaṁ vutte, bhagavā āyasmantaṁ nāgasamālaṁ etadavoca:
> “ayaṁ, nāgasamāla, pantho;
> iminā gacchāmā”ti.

Thus have I heard—
At one time the Blessed One was traveling along the highway in Kosala, accompanied by Venerable Nāgasamāla as his attendant monk (*pacchāsamaṇa*).
Venerable Nāgasamāla saw a fork in the road along the way.
Having seen it, he said to the Blessed One:
"Bhante, this is the path. Let us go this way."
When this was said, the Blessed One said to Venerable Nāgasamāla:
"Nāgasamāla, this is the path. Let us go this way."

> Dutiyampi kho …pe…
> tatiyampi kho āyasmā nāgasamālo bhagavantaṁ etadavoca:
> “ayaṁ, bhante, bhagavā pantho;
> iminā gacchāmā”ti.
> Tatiyampi kho bhagavā āyasmantaṁ nāgasamālaṁ etadavoca:
> “ayaṁ, nāgasamāla, pantho;
> iminā gacchāmā”ti.
> Atha kho āyasmā nāgasamālo bhagavato pattacīvaraṁ tattheva chamāyaṁ nikkhipitvā pakkāmi:
> “idaṁ, bhante, bhagavato pattacīvaran”ti.

A second time...
And a third time, Venerable Nāgasamāla said to the Blessed One:
"Bhante, this is the path. Let us go this way."
And a third time, the Blessed One said to Venerable Nāgasamāla:
"Nāgasamāla, this is the path. Let us go this way."
Then, Venerable Nāgasamāla put down the Blessed One's bowl and robes right there on the ground and walked away, saying:
"Bhante, here is the Blessed One's bowl and outer robe."

> Atha kho āyasmato ngasamālassa tena panthena gacchantassa antarāmagge corā nikkhamitvā hatthehi ca pādehi ca ākoṭesuṁ pattañca bhindiṁsu saṅghāṭiñca vipphālesuṁ.
> Atha kho āyasmā nāgasamālo bhinnena pattena vipphālitāya saṅghāṭiyā yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṁ abhivādetvā ekamantaṁ nisīdi.
> Ekamantaṁ nisinno kho āyasmā nāgasamālo bhagavantaṁ etadavoca:
> “idha mayhaṁ, bhante, tena panthena gacchantassa antarāmagge corā nikkhamitvā hatthehi ca pādehi ca ākoṭesuṁ, pattañca bhindiṁsu, saṅghāṭiñca vipphālesun”ti.

Then, as Venerable Nāgasamāla was going along that path, bandits came out along the way, beat him with their hands and feet, broke his bowl, and tore his outer robe (*saṅghāṭi*).
Then Venerable Nāgasamāla, with his broken bowl and torn outer robe, went to the Blessed One. Having approached and paid respects, he sat down to one side.
As he sat there to one side, Venerable Nāgasamāla said to the Blessed One:
"Bhante, as I was going along that path, bandits came out along the way, beat me with their hands and feet, broke my bowl, and tore my outer robe."

> Atha kho bhagavā etamatthaṁ viditvā tāyaṁ velāyaṁ imaṁ udānaṁ udānesi:
> 
> “Saddhiṁ caramekato vasaṁ,
> Misso aññajanena vedagū;
> Vidvā pajahāti pāpakaṁ,
> Koñco khīrapakova ninnagan”ti.
> 
> Sattamaṁ.

Then the Blessed One, understanding the significance of this, uttered this inspired utterance at that time:

"Wandering together, dwelling as one,
the knower of truth (*vedagū*), though mixed with ordinary people,
the wise one discards what is unwholesome,
like the heron separating milk from water."
(Seventh)

## Translator Notes

1. **Intimate Teacher-to-Student Voice**: The English translation maintains a direct and clear register, using contemporary language to highlight the dramatic and moral weight of the narrative.
2. **Attendant Monk (*Pacchāsamaṇa*)**: Translated as "attendant monk" to clarify Nāgasamāla's role of service to the Buddha, explaining why he was carrying the Buddha's bowl and robes.
3. **The Knower of Truth (*Vedagū*)**: Translated as "knower of truth" to reflect the spiritual quality of an enlightened sage who remains undefiled by their surroundings.
4. **Simile of the Heron (*Koñca*)**: The heron separating milk from water is a classical Indian metaphor for discernment, where the wise person selects only the wholesome (*kusala*) qualities and discards the unwholesome (*pāpaka*).
