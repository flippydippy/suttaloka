# The Enlightenment (First, Middle, and Third Watches of the Night)
## Bodhi Suttas (Ud 1.1, 1.2, 1.3)

## Bodhi Sutta (Ud 1.1) — First Watch of the Night

> Evaṁ me sutaṁ—
> ekaṁ samayaṁ bhagavā uruvelāyaṁ viharati najjā nerañjarāya tīre bodhirukkhamūle paṭhamābhisambuddho.
> Tena kho pana samayena bhagavā sattāhaṁ ekapallaṅkena nisinno hoti vimuttisukhapaṭisaṁvedī.
> Atha kho bhagavā tassa sattāhassa accayena tamhā samādhimhā vuṭṭhahitvā rattiyā paṭhamaṁ yāmaṁ paṭiccasamuppādaṁ anulomaṁ sādhukaṁ manasākāsi:

Thus have I heard—
At one time the Blessed One was staying in Uruvelā, on the bank of the river Nerañjarā, at the foot of the Bodhi tree, having newly attained full enlightenment.
At that time, the Blessed One sat in a single posture for seven days, experiencing the bliss of liberation (*vimuttisukha*).
Then, after those seven days, the Blessed One emerged from that concentration (*samādhi*), and during the first watch of the night, he reflected closely on Dependent Origination (*paṭiccasamuppāda*) in forward order:

> “Iti imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti, imassuppādā idaṁ uppajjati, yadidaṁ—
> avijjāpaccayā saṅkhārā,
> saṅkhārapaccayā viññāṇaṁ,
> viññāṇapaccayā nāmarūpaṁ,
> nāmarūpapaccayā saḷāyatanaṁ,
> saḷāyatanapaccayā phasso,
> phassapaccayā vedanā,
> vedanāpaccayā taṇhā,
> taṇhāpaccayā upādānaṁ,
> upādānapaccayā bhavo,
> bhavapaccayā jāti,
> jātipaccayā jarāmaraṇaṁ sokaparidevadukkhadomanassupāyāsā sambhavanti.
> Evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa samudayo hotī”ti.

"When this is, that is; from the arising of this, that arises. That is to say:
conditioned by ignorance (*avijjā*), mental reactions (*saṅkhāras*) arise;
conditioned by mental reactions, consciousness (*viññāṇa*) arises;
conditioned by consciousness, mind-and-matter (*nāmarūpa*) arises;
conditioned by mind-and-matter, the six sense bases (*saḷāyatana*) arise;
conditioned by the six sense bases, contact (*phassa*) arises;
conditioned by contact, sensations (*vedanās*) arise;
conditioned by sensations, craving (*taṇhā*) arises;
conditioned by craving, clinging (*upādāna*) arises;
conditioned by clinging, becoming (*bhava*) arises;
conditioned by becoming, birth (*jāti*) arises;
conditioned by birth, decay and death (*jarāmaraṇa*), sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair arise.
Thus is the arising of this entire mass of suffering (*dukkha*)."

> Atha kho bhagavā etamatthaṁ viditvā tāyaṁ velāyaṁ imaṁ udānaṁ udānesi:
> 
> “Yadā have pātubhavanti dhammā,
> Ātāpino jhāyato brāhmaṇassa;
> Athassa kaṅkhā vapayanti sabbā,
> Yato pajānāti sahetudhamman”ti.
> 
> Paṭhamaṁ.

Then, the Blessed One, understanding the significance of this, uttered this inspired utterance at that time:
"When the laws of nature become clear indeed
to the ardent (*ātāpī*), meditating Brahmin,
then all his doubts vanish,
since he understands each phenomenon with its cause."
(First)

## Bodhi Sutta (Ud 1.2) — Middle Watch of the Night

> Evaṁ me sutaṁ—
> ekaṁ samayaṁ bhagavā uruvelāyaṁ viharati najjā nerañjarāya tīre bodhirukkhamūle paṭhamābhisambuddho.
> Tena kho pana samayena bhagavā sattāhaṁ ekapallaṅkena nisinno hoti vimuttisukhapaṭisaṁvedī.
> Atha kho bhagavā tassa sattāhassa accayena tamhā samādhimhā vuṭṭhahitvā rattiyā majjhimaṁ yāmaṁ paṭiccasamuppādaṁ paṭilomaṁ sādhukaṁ manasākāsi:

Thus have I heard—
At one time the Blessed One was staying in Uruvelā, on the bank of the river Nerañjarā, at the foot of the Bodhi tree, having newly attained full enlightenment.
At that time, the Blessed One sat in a single posture for seven days, experiencing the bliss of liberation (*vimuttisukha*).
Then, after those seven days, the Blessed One emerged from that concentration (*samādhi*), and during the middle watch of the night, he reflected closely on Dependent Origination (*paṭiccasamuppāda*) in reverse order:

> “Iti imasmiṁ asati idaṁ na hoti, imassa nirodhā idaṁ nirujjhati, yadidaṁ—
> avijjānirodhā saṅkhāranirodho,
> saṅkhāranirodhā viññāṇanirodho,
> viññāṇanirodhā nāmarūpanirodho,
> nāmarūpanirodhā saḷāyatananirodho,
> saḷāyatananirodhā phassanirodho,
> phassanirodhā vedanānirodho,
> vedanānirodhā taṇhānirodho,
> taṇhānirodhā upādānanirodho,
> upādānanirodhā bhavanirodho,
> bhavanirodhā jātinirodho,
> jātinirodhā jarāmaraṇaṁ sokaparidevadukkhadomanassupāyāsā nirujjhanti.
> Evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa nirodho hotī”ti.

"When this is not, that is not; from the cessation of this, that ceases. That is to say:
with the cessation of ignorance (*avijjā*), mental reactions (*saṅkhāras*) cease;
with the cessation of mental reactions, consciousness (*viññāṇa*) ceases;
with the cessation of consciousness, mind-and-matter (*nāmarūpa*) ceases;
with the cessation of mind-and-matter, the six sense bases (*saḷāyatana*) cease;
with the cessation of the six sense bases, contact (*phassa*) ceases;
with the cessation of contact, sensations (*vedanās*) cease;
with the cessation of sensations, craving (*taṇhā*) ceases;
with the cessation of craving, clinging (*upādāna*) ceases;
with the cessation of clinging, becoming (*bhava*) ceases;
with the cessation of becoming, birth (*jāti*) ceases;
with the cessation of birth, decay and death (*jarāmaraṇa*), sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair cease.
Thus is the cessation of this entire mass of suffering (*dukkha*)."

> Atha kho bhagavā etamatthaṁ viditvā tāyaṁ velāyaṁ imaṁ udānaṁ udānesi:
> 
> “Yadā have pātubhavanti dhammā,
> Ātāpino jhāyato brāhmaṇassa;
> Athassa kaṅkhā vapayanti sabbā,
> Yato khayaṁ paccayānaṁ avedī”ti.
> 
> Dutiyaṁ.

Then, the Blessed One, understanding the significance of this, uttered this inspired utterance at that time:
"When the laws of nature become clear indeed
to the ardent (*ātāpī*), meditating Brahmin,
then all his doubts vanish,
since he has realized the ending of all causes."
(Second)

## Bodhi Sutta (Ud 1.3) — Third Watch of the Night

> Evaṁ me sutaṁ—
> ekaṁ samayaṁ bhagavā uruvelāyaṁ viharati najjā nerañjarāya tīre bodhirukkhamūle paṭhamābhisambuddho.
> Tena kho pana samayena bhagavā sattāhaṁ ekapallaṅkena nisinno hoti vimuttisukhapaṭisaṁvedī.
> Atha kho bhagavā tassa sattāhassa accayena tamhā samādhimhā vuṭṭhahitvā rattiyā pacchimaṁ yāmaṁ paṭiccasamuppādaṁ anulomapaṭilomaṁ sādhukaṁ manasākāsi:

Thus have I heard—
At one time the Blessed One was staying in Uruvelā, on the bank of the river Nerañjarā, at the foot of the Bodhi tree, having newly attained full enlightenment.
At that time, the Blessed One sat in a single posture for seven days, experiencing the bliss of liberation (*vimuttisukha*).
Then, after those seven days, the Blessed One emerged from that concentration (*samādhi*), and during the third watch of the night, he reflected closely on Dependent Origination (*paṭiccasamuppāda*) in forward and reverse order:

> “Iti imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti, imassuppādā idaṁ uppajjati,
> imasmiṁ asati idaṁ na hoti, imassa nirodhā idaṁ nirujjhati; yadidaṁ—
> avijjāpaccayā saṅkhārā,
> saṅkhārapaccayā viññāṇaṁ,
> viññāṇapaccayā nāmarūpaṁ,
> nāmarūpapaccayā saḷāyatanaṁ,
> saḷāyatanapaccayā phasso,
> phassapaccayā vedanā,
> vedanāpaccayā taṇhā,
> taṇhāpaccayā upādānaṁ,
> upādānapaccayā bhavo,
> bhavapaccayā jāti,
> jātipaccayā jarāmaraṇaṁ sokaparidevadukkhadomanassupāyāsā sambhavanti.
> Evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa samudayo hoti.

"When this is, that is; from the arising of this, that arises.
When this is not, that is not; from the cessation of this, that ceases. That is to say:
conditioned by ignorance (*avijjā*), mental reactions (*saṅkhāras*) arise;
conditioned by mental reactions, consciousness (*viññāṇa*) arises;
conditioned by consciousness, mind-and-matter (*nāmarūpa*) arises;
conditioned by mind-and-matter, the six sense bases (*saḷāyatana*) arise;
conditioned by the six sense bases, contact (*phassa*) arises;
conditioned by contact, sensations (*vedanās*) arise;
conditioned by sensations, craving (*taṇhā*) arises;
conditioned by craving, clinging (*upādāna*) arises;
conditioned by clinging, becoming (*bhava*) arises;
conditioned by becoming, birth (*jāti*) arises;
conditioned by birth, decay and death (*jarāmaraṇa*), sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair arise.
Thus is the arising of this entire mass of suffering (*dukkha*).

> Avijjāya tveva asesavirāganirodhā saṅkhāranirodho,
> saṅkhāranirodhā viññāṇanirodho,
> viññāṇanirodhā nāmarūpanirodho,
> nāmarūpanirodhā saḷāyatananirodho,
> saḷāyatananirodhā phassanirodho,
> phassanirodhā vedanānirodho,
> vedanānirodhā taṇhānirodho,
> taṇhānirodhā upādānanirodho,
> upādānanirodhā bhavanirodho,
> bhavanirodhā jātinirodho,
> jātinirodhā jarāmaraṇaṁ sokaparidevadukkhadomanassupāyāsā nirujjhanti.
> Evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa nirodho hotī”ti.

But with the complete fading away and cessation of ignorance (*avijjā*), mental reactions (*saṅkhāras*) cease;
with the cessation of mental reactions, consciousness (*viññāṇa*) ceases;
with the cessation of consciousness, mind-and-matter (*nāmarūpa*) ceases;
with the cessation of mind-and-matter, the six sense bases (*saḷāyatana*) cease;
with the cessation of the six sense bases, contact (*phassa*) ceases;
with the cessation of contact, sensations (*vedanās*) cease;
with the cessation of sensations, craving (*taṇhā*) ceases;
with the cessation of craving, clinging (*upādāna*) ceases;
with the cessation of clinging, becoming (*bhava*) ceases;
with the cessation of becoming, birth (*jāti*) ceases;
with the cessation of birth, decay and death (*jarāmaraṇa*), sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair cease.
Thus is the cessation of this entire mass of suffering (*dukkha*)."

> Atha kho bhagavā etamatthaṁ viditvā tāyaṁ velāyaṁ imaṁ udānaṁ udānesi:
> 
> “Yadā have pātubhavanti dhammā,
> Ātāpino jhāyato brāhmaṇassa;
> Vidhūpayaṁ tiṭṭhati mārasenaṁ,
> Sūriyova obhāsayamantalikkhan”ti.
> 
> Tatiyaṁ.

Then, the Blessed One, understanding the significance of this, uttered this inspired utterance at that time:
"When the laws of nature become clear indeed
to the ardent (*ātāpī*), meditating Brahmin,
he stands dispelling the army of Māra,
like the sun illuminating the sky."
(Third)

## Translator Notes

1. **Intimate Teacher-to-Student Voice**: The English translation adopts the direct, experiential tone of the S.N. Goenka Vipassana tradition. We use simple, clear, and practical terms to connect with a meditator's daily practice, avoiding dry scholastic or formal academic English.
2. **Mind-and-Matter vs. Name-and-Form**: Translated *nāmarūpa* as "mind-and-matter" (rather than "name-and-form") to align with Goenkaji's experiential framing of the physical and mental processes and how they interact.
3. **Sensations for *Vedanā***: In accordance with the Goenka anchor guidelines, *vedanā* is rendered strictly as "sensations" (not "feelings") to emphasize the bodily, somatic basis of reactivity in the dependent origination chain.
4. **Brahmin Register**: Translated *brāhmaṇa* as "Brahmin" but with the context of a purified, meditating seeker, in line with the tradition's universal (non-sectarian) redefinition of the term.
5. **Reverse Order (*Paṭiloma*) and Cessation (*Nirodha*)**: In the second watch of the night, the Buddha reflected on the cessation aspect of dependent origination (*paṭiloma*), showing how dismantling the root cause (ignorance) systematically resolves the entire chain of suffering. We use "cessation" (*nirodha*) to represent active dismantling rather than passive termination.
6. **Ending of Causes (*Khayaṁ paccayānaṁ*)**: Translated as "the ending of all causes" to reflect the experiential realization of how the causal conditions for suffering are completely extinguished and broken.
7. **Forward and Reverse Order (*Anulomapaṭiloma*)**: In the final watch of the night, the Buddha integrated both aspects of dependent origination, observing both the arising (*anuloma*) and the cessation (*paṭiloma*) of the entire chain of suffering.
8. **The Army of Māra (*Mārasena*)**: The personification of defilements (craving, aversion, ignorance, fear). Dispelling Māra's army represents the total eradication of mental impurities.
9. **Simile of the Sun (*Sūriya*)**: The dispelling of defilements is compared to the sun breaking through and lighting up the sky, representing the sudden and complete illumination of wisdom over ignorance.
